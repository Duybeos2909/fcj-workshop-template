Event 1

Summary Report: “FCJ Community Day – Stop Procrastinating, Start Building”

Event Information

  • Event name: FCJ Community Day – Stop Procrastinating, Start Building
  • Date: Saturday, May 30, 2026
  • Time: 09:00 – 12:00
  • Location: Bitexco Financial Tower, Ho Chi Minh City
  • Event type: Community sharing, personal development, systems thinking, and AI product demonstration

Event Overview

The FCJ Community Day – Stop Procrastinating, Start Building event focused on the relationship between systems thinking, procrastination, emotional regulation, and the process of turning ideas into practical products.

Unlike a conventional technology workshop that concentrates mainly on programming languages, cloud services, or system implementation, this event explored the mindset and behavioral patterns that directly influence a developer’s ability to learn, solve problems, and complete projects.

The event introduced the Iceberg Model as a framework for analyzing complex problems beyond their visible symptoms. It also explained the psychological mechanism behind procrastination through the Guilt Loop, presented scientific perspectives on emotional regulation, and encouraged participants to take action without waiting for perfect conditions.

Another important part of the program was the demonstration of Tử vi Đại Việt, an AI-powered platform that combines artificial intelligence with traditional Vietnamese knowledge areas such as astrology, numerology, feng shui, physiognomy, tarot, and star maps.

Speakers and Contributors

The program included contributions from:

  • A speaker presenting the Iceberg Model and Systems Thinking.
  • The Tử vi Đại Việt Team, developers of an AI-powered Vietnamese astrology platform.
  • Members of the FCJ AI Community, who shared practical experiences, product-building lessons, and career-development perspectives.

Event Objectives

The event aimed to help participants:

  • Understand the Iceberg Model as a tool for analyzing complex and recurring problems.
  • Distinguish between visible symptoms, repeated patterns, underlying structures, and mental models.
  • Recognize the limitations of reactive problem-solving.
  • Understand procrastination as an emotional regulation issue rather than only a time-management problem.
  • Identify the psychological cycle that reinforces avoidance and self-blame.
  • Learn practical methods for starting difficult tasks with smaller and more manageable actions.
  • Explore how artificial intelligence can be applied to specialized cultural and non-technical domains.
  • Encourage developers to start building projects instead of waiting for ideal skills, resources, or conditions.
  • Strengthen the connection between technical ability, personal discipline, problem-solving mindset, and product development.

Key Highlights

The Iceberg Model: Looking Beneath the Surface

The first major topic introduced the Iceberg Model, a common framework in Systems Thinking.

The model suggests that the events people observe are only the visible part of a much larger system. Beneath those events are repeated patterns, structural causes, relationships, assumptions, and mental models that continuously influence outcomes.

The main layers can be understood as follows:

  • Events: What has just happened and can be directly observed.
  • Patterns and trends: Behaviors or incidents that repeatedly occur over time.
  • System structures: Processes, policies, dependencies, incentives, and relationships that create repeated patterns.
  • Mental models: Beliefs, assumptions, expectations, and ways of thinking that influence decisions and system design.

In software engineering, a visible error may be only the event at the surface. The actual cause might involve:

  • Unclear requirements.
  • Weak communication between team members.
  • Poor ownership of system components.
  • Missing automated tests.
  • Tight coupling between services.
  • Incomplete monitoring.
  • An unsuitable development workflow.
  • Repeated technical debt.
  • Incorrect assumptions made during architecture design.

The session emphasized that resolving a visible bug does not necessarily prevent the same class of problem from returning. A more effective engineer should examine why the surrounding system allowed the problem to occur repeatedly.

Why Solving Symptoms Is Not Enough

The event compared Traditional Thinking with Systems Thinking.

Traditional or reactive thinking usually follows this pattern:

  1. A problem appears.
  2. The team attempts to remove the immediate symptom.
  3. The system temporarily returns to normal.
  4. The same or a related problem appears again.
  5. The team repeats the same firefighting process.

This approach may be necessary during urgent incidents, but it does not create long-term improvement when used as the only problem-solving method.

Systems Thinking asks deeper questions:

  • Why does this problem continue to occur?
  • Which process or dependency creates this behavior?
  • What patterns existed before the incident?
  • Which incentives or constraints influence the system?
  • What assumptions were made during the original design?
  • Which change could improve the whole system rather than one isolated symptom?

A key message from this section was that developers should not only ask how to fix an incident, but also understand why the system repeatedly creates the conditions for that incident.

This perspective is useful in:

  • Root-cause analysis.
  • Software debugging.
  • Architecture reviews.
  • Incident retrospectives.
  • Team workflow improvement.
  • Security assessments.
  • Performance optimization.
  • Product requirement analysis.

The Cost of Waiting: Understanding the Guilt Loop

The event then analyzed procrastination through a cycle referred to as the Guilt Loop.

A typical procrastination cycle may occur as follows:

  1. A person faces a difficult, unclear, or emotionally uncomfortable task.
  2. The task creates fear, uncertainty, boredom, pressure, or self-doubt.
  3. The person avoids the task by using social media, watching videos, playing games, sleeping, or doing easier work.
  4. Avoidance provides temporary emotional relief.
  5. The deadline becomes closer, while the original task remains unfinished.
  6. Stress and fatigue increase.
  7. The person experiences guilt, disappointment, and self-criticism.
  8. These negative emotions make the task feel even more difficult.
  9. The person continues avoiding it, causing the cycle to repeat.

This explanation reframed procrastination. Instead of seeing it only as laziness or weak scheduling, the session presented it as a behavior connected to emotional avoidance.

The event highlighted an important message:

The perfect moment never arrives. Every day of waiting makes the iceberg larger and more difficult to break.

This message encouraged participants to stop expecting motivation, confidence, or ideal conditions to appear before beginning a task.

Interactive Examples of Procrastination

The speaker used relatable questions to make the topic more practical, including situations such as:

  • Having a deadline the following day but continuing to scroll through social media.
  • Delaying an important assignment because the first step feels unclear.
  • Waiting for the perfect project idea before starting to build.
  • Avoiding communication because of fear of rejection or embarrassment.
  • Repeatedly planning without producing an actual result.

These examples helped participants recognize that procrastination can affect both technical and personal decisions.

Tử vi Đại Việt: AI-Powered Astrology Platform

A live demonstration introduced Tử vi Đại Việt, an AI-powered platform that applies modern artificial intelligence to traditional Vietnamese cultural knowledge.

The platform combines multiple knowledge domains, including:

  • Vietnamese astrology.
  • Numerology.
  • Feng shui.
  • Physiognomy.
  • Tarot.
  • Star maps.
  • Traditional interpretive knowledge.
  • Psychological and emotional context.

The project demonstrated that AI products do not need to focus only on common technical areas such as code generation, customer support, or document summarization. AI can also be applied to specialized knowledge domains with strong cultural value and niche user demand.

The team presented a future development roadmap that may include:

  • Expanding the knowledge base.
  • Cross-validating results between multiple knowledge systems.
  • Introducing AI Voice Mode.
  • Adding a psychological layer for more emotionally relevant responses.
  • Improving personalization.
  • Creating more natural user interactions.

The demonstration also provided a useful product-development lesson: successful AI applications can emerge from combining technical capability with deep domain knowledge.

The Hidden Data: The Science of Procrastination

The psychology section explained that procrastination is often associated with difficulty managing emotions.

The presentation referred to the idea that approximately 70–80% of procrastination may originate from emotional regulation difficulties, while a large percentage of adults experience procrastination in at least one important area of life.

The session described the interaction between:

  • The Prefrontal Cortex, associated with planning, logic, long-term decision-making, and self-control.
  • The Limbic System, associated with emotional reactions, fear, immediate comfort, and reward-seeking behavior.

When a task creates fear, uncertainty, or emotional discomfort, the immediate emotional response may overpower the logical understanding that the task should be completed.

This explains why a person can fully understand the consequences of delaying a task while still choosing short-term relief.

The discussion encouraged participants to manage the emotional entry point of a task by:

  • Reducing its initial size.
  • Clarifying the first action.
  • Accepting discomfort.
  • Avoiding perfectionism.
  • Creating a short and achievable work session.
  • Building confidence through repeated completion.

Don’t Let the Iceberg Freeze Your Future

The final session delivered a direct call to action:

Don’t let the iceberg freeze your future. Go build, starting today.

The closing message emphasized that progress should begin with action rather than perfect preparation.

Three practical principles were discussed:

Build One Small Habit

Large goals often become overwhelming. A small repeatable habit makes it easier to create momentum.

Examples include:

  • Writing code for 20 minutes each day.
  • Completing one small feature.
  • Reading one section of technical documentation.
  • Fixing one issue in a project.
  • Writing one paragraph of a report.

Normalize Fear and Uncertainty

Fear does not always indicate that a task should be avoided. It may indicate that the task is meaningful, unfamiliar, or connected to personal growth.

Developers should expect uncertainty when:

  • Learning a new technology.
  • Presenting a project.
  • Joining a new team.
  • Applying for a job.
  • Building a product without complete knowledge.
  • Receiving technical feedback.

Start with a Small Idea

A small working product creates more learning value than a large idea that remains unfinished.

Participants were encouraged to:

  • Build a basic version first.
  • Validate the core workflow.
  • Learn from feedback.
  • Improve gradually.
  • Avoid unnecessary perfection during the initial stage.

Key Takeaways

Systems Thinking

Through the Iceberg Model, I learned that technical problems should not be evaluated only through their visible symptoms.

A recurring bug may reflect deeper problems such as:

  • Weak system design.
  • Missing validation.
  • Poor communication.
  • Unclear responsibility.
  • Insufficient monitoring.
  • Unmanaged technical debt.
  • Incorrect development assumptions.

Systems Thinking helps identify the relationships and structures that repeatedly generate these problems.

Root-Cause Analysis

The event reinforced the importance of asking deeper questions when troubleshooting:

  • What changed before the problem occurred?
  • Has this behavior appeared previously?
  • Which system component influences the result?
  • Is the issue caused by code, process, communication, configuration, or architecture?
  • Which solution prevents recurrence rather than only removing the current symptom?

This approach can improve the quality of technical decisions and reduce repeated firefighting.

Procrastination and Emotional Regulation

I learned that procrastination is not always caused by poor time management.

It may be driven by:

  • Fear of failure.
  • Fear of producing low-quality work.
  • Unclear requirements.
  • Perfectionism.
  • Lack of confidence.
  • Emotional discomfort.
  • A task that appears too large.

Understanding the Guilt Loop makes it easier to recognize avoidance before it becomes a repeated habit.

Building Confidence Through Action

Confidence does not always come before action. In many cases, confidence develops after a person completes small actions consistently.

Instead of waiting to feel fully prepared, it is more effective to:

  • Begin with a limited task.
  • Produce a basic result.
  • Review the outcome.
  • Learn from mistakes.
  • Repeat the process.

Artificial Intelligence and Specialized Domains

The Tử vi Đại Việt demonstration expanded my understanding of AI product opportunities.

AI can be combined with:

  • Cultural knowledge.
  • Traditional practices.
  • Psychology.
  • Education.
  • Personal guidance.
  • Specialized professional knowledge.
  • Niche user communities.

The value of an AI product depends not only on the model being used, but also on the quality of the domain knowledge, user experience, and problem being solved.

Applying the Knowledge to Work

Applying the Iceberg Model to Debugging

When a software problem occurs, I can apply the Iceberg Model through four levels:

  1. Event: Identify the current error.
  2. Pattern: Determine whether it has happened before.
  3. Structure: Examine architecture, processes, dependencies, and team workflow.
  4. Mental model: Review the assumptions that influenced the implementation.

For example, repeated API failures may not be caused only by one Lambda function. The deeper issue may involve:

  • Incorrect timeout settings.
  • Poor retry logic.
  • Synchronous dependencies.
  • Missing queue-based processing.
  • Weak monitoring.
  • Unrealistic traffic assumptions.

Applying Systems Thinking to Architecture Design

When designing an AWS Serverless solution, Systems Thinking can help evaluate how services affect one another.

Instead of selecting services separately, the design should consider:

  • Request flow.
  • Authentication.
  • Tenant isolation.
  • Error handling.
  • Retry behavior.
  • Data consistency.
  • Security boundaries.
  • Monitoring.
  • Operational cost.
  • Future scalability.

This is especially relevant to the Smart Attendance SaaS project because its architecture includes multiple interconnected services such as API Gateway, Lambda, DynamoDB, EventBridge, SQS, SES, and CloudWatch.

Breaking the Guilt Loop During Project Work

When a task feels too difficult, I can reduce avoidance by defining a smaller first step.

For example:

  • Instead of “complete the architecture,” define “draw the authentication flow.”
  • Instead of “finish the report,” define “write the introduction.”
  • Instead of “build the backend,” define “create one check-in endpoint.”
  • Instead of “learn DynamoDB,” define “design one partition-key pattern.”

This makes the task clearer and reduces the emotional pressure associated with starting.

Applying Lessons from Tử vi Đại Việt

The product demonstration showed that technical solutions become more valuable when they are connected to a specific user group and a meaningful domain.

For future projects, I should evaluate:

  • Who has the problem?
  • Why do existing tools fail to solve it?
  • What unique knowledge can be integrated?
  • How can AI improve the experience?
  • What is the smallest useful version of the product?

Attending FCJ Community Day – Stop Procrastinating, Start Building was a meaningful experience because the event connected technical problem-solving with developer psychology and personal discipline.

It did not treat technical growth as a process based only on learning more tools. Instead, it demonstrated that progress also depends on how developers understand systems, respond to uncertainty, manage emotions, and consistently take action.

A More Complete View of Problem-Solving

The Iceberg Model gave me a more structured method for analyzing complex issues.

Before this event, it was easy to think of debugging as identifying an error and correcting the related code. The session showed that recurring incidents may reflect broader problems involving architecture, process, communication, assumptions, or ownership.

This perspective is useful not only for software development but also for teamwork and personal learning.

A Better Understanding of Procrastination

The psychological explanation of procrastination was one of the most practical parts of the event.

It helped me understand why knowing that a task is important does not automatically make it easier to begin. Emotional discomfort, fear of failure, and perfectionism can create avoidance even when the consequences are clear.

The concept of the Guilt Loop provided a useful framework for recognizing this behavior and interrupting it before it becomes more serious.

Tử vi Đại Việt was an interesting example of combining artificial intelligence with traditional Vietnamese cultural knowledge.

The product showed that innovation does not always require inventing an entirely new field. It can also come from applying modern technology to an existing domain in a more accessible, personalized, and scalable way.

This encouraged me to think more broadly about possible AI applications.

Motivation to Start Building

The closing message was simple but relevant:

Go build, starting today.

It reminded me that technical improvement requires more than planning, watching tutorials, or waiting for confidence. Actual progress begins when an idea is converted into a small working result.

The event encouraged me to start with what I currently know, accept incomplete conditions, and improve through continuous implementation.
